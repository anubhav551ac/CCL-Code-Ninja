.01 — Type of Compost
Type of Compost
List out which waste can be used for which type of compost. Different organic materials produce different compost qualities.
  • Food scraps → Rich nitrogen compost (Green material)
  • Dry leaves, cardboard → Carbon compost (Brown material)
  • Grass clippings → Nitrogen-rich liquid feed
  • Wood ash → Potassium booster for alkaline soil
  • Coffee grounds → Acidic compost for acid-loving plants

.02 — Estimated Time
Estimated Time
How long each waste item takes to fully decompose into usable compost under average home composting conditions.
  • Fruit & vegetable scraps → 2 – 5 weeks
  • Coffee grounds / tea bags → 2 – 3 weeks
  • Grass clippings → 1 – 3 weeks
  • Dry leaves → 6 months – 1 year
  • Cardboard / newspaper → 2 – 6 months
  • Eggshells → 6 months – 2 years
  • Wood chips / sawdust → 1 – 2 years

.03 — Non Degradable
Non Degradable
Waste that cannot be turned into compost. These items must be recycled, disposed of properly, or taken to a special facility.
  • Plastic bags & packaging
  • Glass bottles & ceramics
  • Metal cans & aluminium foil
  • Synthetic fabrics & rubber
  • Batteries & electronics
  • Treated or painted wood
  • Coated or laminated paper

How to Start Composting at Home

.04
.01

Choose a Bin

Use an outdoor compost heap, a tumbler bin, or an indoor worm farm depending on your space and volume of waste.

.02

Layer Your Materials

Alternate green (nitrogen-rich) and brown (carbon-rich) layers. Aim for a 1:3 green-to-brown ratio for best results.

.03

Keep It Moist

Your compost should feel like a damp sponge. Too dry — add water or green material. Too wet — add dry browns or cardboard.

.04

Turn & Wait

Turn your pile every 1–2 weeks to add oxygen. In 6–12 weeks you'll have rich, dark compost ready to feed your garden.
